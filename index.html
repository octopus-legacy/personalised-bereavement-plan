Read to caller:

Sometimes a death is referred to the coroner - that can happen if it was sudden, unexpected, or if the doctors aren't certain of the cause. It doesn't mean anything is wrong, it's just a legal process that needs to happen in certain circumstances.

If that does apply, the coroner's office will keep you updated and they'll issue an Interim Certificate so you can start letting organisations know in the meantime. It just means the registration might take a little longer than usual, but there's nothing you need to worry about.

Read to caller:

So there's a really useful free service called Tell Us Once. Basically, instead of having to contact lots of different government departments individually to let them know about the death, you can do it all in one go. It takes about 15 minutes and it covers places like HMRC, the DWP, the Passport Office, DVLA, and your local council.

You can usually set it up when you register the death - the registrar will give you a reference number, and then you complete it either online or over the phone. You've got 28 days from when the death is registered to do it.

You'll just need a few bits of information to hand - things like their National Insurance number, passport number, and driving licence number. We'll pop all the details and a link into your support plan so you don't need to remember any of this now.

Bereavement rights at work

"You do have some rights around time off, and it's worth checking what your employer offers on top of the legal minimum. Would you like me to include a summary of this in your plan?"

Here's a summary of the key rights around bereavement and work:

Parental Bereavement Leave (Jack's Law)

If you've lost a child under 18 (or had a stillbirth after 24 weeks), you're entitled to 2 weeks' statutory leave, regardless of how long you've been with your employer. This can be taken as one block or two separate weeks, any time within 56 weeks of the death. You're also entitled to Statutory Parental Bereavement Pay (currently £184.03/week or 90% of average earnings, whichever is lower) if you've been employed for at least 26 weeks.

Time Off for Dependants

All employees have a right to reasonable unpaid time off to deal with an emergency involving a dependant. This includes making funeral arrangements and dealing with immediate matters after a death. There's no set limit on how long, but it's intended for the immediate emergency, not extended leave.

Compassionate / Bereavement Leave

Beyond the statutory rights above, there is no general legal right to paid bereavement leave in the UK. However, many employers offer compassionate leave as part of their contract or employee handbook, typically between 3-5 days paid. It's worth checking your employment contract, staff handbook, or speaking to HR.

Sick Leave

If grief is affecting your health, your GP can sign you off work. You'd be entitled to Statutory Sick Pay (currently £116.75/week for up to 28 weeks), and your employer may offer enhanced sick pay on top of this.

Flexible Working

You have the right to request flexible working from day one of employment. This could include adjusted hours, working from home, or a temporary change to your pattern while you deal with things. Your employer must consider the request reasonably.

Protection from Unfair Treatment

You cannot be dismissed or treated unfairly for taking time off for dependants or parental bereavement leave. If you feel you're being pressured to return before you're ready, you may want to speak to ACAS (0300 123 1100) or Citizens Advice for free confidential support.

Probate & Legal

We can help you understand the legal side of things
▾
Read to caller:
Probate is simply the legal process of sorting out someone's money, property and belongings after they've passed away. It gives the right person - usually a family member or someone named in the will - the legal authority to access bank accounts, sell property, pay any debts, and pass on what's left to the right people.

Not every estate needs probate. If everything was held in joint names, it may pass automatically. But if the person had savings, property, or investments in their name alone, probate is usually needed before anyone can access those.

You don't need to worry about this right now - it's something we can help you understand when the time comes. There's no rush.
